Complex numbers

A clear brief: the plane, i, polar form, and why they matter

What is a complex number?

A complex number is an ordered pair of real numbers written as z = a + bi, where a is the real part and b is the imaginary part. You can think of it as a single point in a 2D plane: move a along the horizontal axis and b along the vertical axis.

This “complex plane” (often called the Argand diagram) is the same idea as Cartesian (x,y) coordinates, with the horizontal axis labelled “real” and the vertical axis labelled “imaginary.”

How to picture z=a+bi: treat it as the point with coordinates (a,b) in that plane. The modulus |z| is the ordinary straight-line distance from the origin to that point—the same length as the hypotenuse of the right triangle whose legs run along the axes from (0,0) to meet at (a,b).

That coordinate view is the right starting point. The symbol i is not just a label, though: it fixes the rules of multiplication, so distances and angles combine in a way that matches rotation and scaling in the plane (see below). So you get both familiar geometry and useful algebra in one package.

What is i?

The symbol i denotes the imaginary unit: a number defined by the property

\displaystyle i^2 = -1

There is no real number whose square is -1, so we extend the number system once, in a controlled way, by adjoining i. Then bi means “b times that unit,” and every complex number a + bi is built from ordinary real addition and multiplication, using the rule above whenever i^2 appears.

Powers of i cycle: i^0=1,\ i^1=i,\ i^2=-1,\ i^3=-i,\ i^4=1,\ \ldots

Polar form: modulus r and argument \theta

Polar form describes the same number z=a+bi by how far it sits from the origin and which way it points. The distance is the modulus. It is written r or |z|—those symbols mean the same thing.

Concretely, r = |z| is the length of the segment from (0,0) to (a,b). In the right triangle formed by the real axis, the vertical side up to (a,b), and that slanted segment, the slanted side is the hypotenuse; its length is r. If you think of circles centred at the origin, r is also the radius of the circle on which z lies. Pythagoras gives the value from the coordinates:

\displaystyle r = |z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}

The angle measured from the positive real axis to the segment joining the origin to z is the argument \theta = \arg(z). By convention, the principal value of the argument is taken with \theta \in \left(-\pi,\,\pi\right] (radians: -\pi < \theta \le \pi; an open cut on the negative real axis, so each direction from the origin has one standard angle). In the right triangle formed by the real axis, the vertical side to (a,b), and the hypotenuse of length r, you have a = r\cos\theta and b = r\sin\theta, so

\displaystyle z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)

Addition

Complex numbers add componentwise, like vectors in the plane:

\displaystyle (a+bi) + (c+di) = (a+c) + (b+d)i

Geometrically, that is the usual “parallelogram rule” for adding vectors from the origin. For a self-contained introduction to vectors in the plane—what they are, how to add them, how scalar multiplication scales and flips them, and how the dot product combines two vectors into a number—see Vectors in the plane — a beginner’s guide.

Multiplication

Algebraically, multiply as for polynomials and use i^2=-1:

\displaystyle (a+bi)(c+di) = (ac-bd) + (ad+bc)i

A beautiful thing happens in polar form. Write each factor as a length times a direction, z_k = r_k e^{i\theta_k}. Then multiplying the two numbers multiplies their lengths and adds their angles—the same law as for real exponentials, now for turns in the plane: e^{i\theta_1} e^{i\theta_2} = e^{i(\theta_1+\theta_2)}. So the product has modulus r_1 r_2 and argument \theta_1 + \theta_2 (up to adding 2\pi if you need another branch). The spread-out rectangular formula above is doing this geometry in disguise.

Worked example: multiplying two complex numbers

  1. Let z_1 = 2 + 3i and z_2 = 1 - 4i.
  2. Expand: (2+3i)(1-4i)=2-8i+3i-12i^2.
  3. Use i^2=-1 carefully: the term -12i^2 means -12\times(i^2). Substitute i^2=-1 to get -12\times(-1)=+12, so the line becomes 2-8i+3i+12.
  4. Collect real and imaginary terms: (2+12)+(-8i+3i)=14-5i.
  5. So z_1z_2=14-5i. (Checks formula: ac-bd=2\cdot1-3(-4)=14, ad+bc=2(-4)+3(1)=-5.)

Worked example: scale then rotate (same numbers)

  1. Start with z_1=2+3i and z_2=1-4i.
  2. Lengths: |z_1|=\sqrt{2^2+3^2}=\sqrt{13}\approx3.606, |z_2|=\sqrt{1^2+(-4)^2}=\sqrt{17}\approx4.123.
  3. Angles: \theta_1=\arg(z_1)=\operatorname{atan2}(3,2)\approx0.983 rad, \theta_2=\arg(z_2)=\operatorname{atan2}(-4,1)\approx-1.326 rad.
  4. Scale rule: |z_1z_2|=|z_1||z_2|=\sqrt{13}\sqrt{17}=\sqrt{221}\approx14.866.
  5. Rotation rule: \arg(z_1z_2)=\theta_1+\theta_2\approx0.983+(-1.326)=-0.343 rad.
  6. Check with rectangular result: z_1z_2=14-5i, so |14-5i|=\sqrt{221} and \arg(14-5i)=\operatorname{atan2}(-5,14)\approx-0.343 rad (same values).

Stage 1: original vectors z_1 and z_2.

\displaystyle r_1 e^{i\theta_1} \cdot r_2 e^{i\theta_2} = r_1 r_2\, e^{i(\theta_1+\theta_2)}

Rotation and scaling. Picture each complex number as an arrow from the origin. Multiplying z_1 by z_2 stretches that first arrow by |z_2| and rotates it about the origin through \theta_2, so it ends up pointing along \theta_1+\theta_2. That is why multiplication “mixes” real and imaginary parts: the algebra is bookkeeping, but the geometry is turn plus scale. If |z_2|=1, there is no stretch—multiplication by z_2 is a pure rotation of the plane (the unit complex numbers are exactly the rotations about the origin). If z_2 is a positive real, you only rescale along the same ray. More generally, multiplying the whole plane by a fixed w applies the same factor |w| and the same turn \arg(w) to every point from the origin—one of the reasons complex numbers are so useful in waves, circuits, and linear systems.

Euler’s formula and e^{i\theta}

The exponential with an imaginary argument ties polar form to analysis:

\displaystyle e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta

So any nonzero z can be written z = r e^{i\theta} with r=|z| and \theta=\arg(z). Multiplying by e^{i\theta} rotates by \theta; in particular e^{i\pi}+1=0 links e,\ i,\ \pi,\ 1 in one identity. That is the same “add the angles in the exponent” rule as in the multiplication diagram above.

Why complex numbers matter

  • Quantum mechanics. Wavefunctions are complex-valued; probabilities come from moduli squared (|\psi|^2). Phase matters for interference.
  • Electrical engineering. Phasors simplify AC circuits: capacitors and inductors become multiplications by complex impedances.
  • Signal processing. The Fourier transform naturally lives on complex exponentials e^{i\omega t}, separating magnitude and phase.
  • Control and dynamics. Eigenvalues of linear systems can be complex; they encode oscillation frequency and damping.
  • Pure mathematics. The fundamental theorem of algebra: every non-constant polynomial with complex coefficients has a root in \mathbb{C}.

A little history

Renaissance algebraists hit equations like x^2+1=0 when solving cubics. Cardano (16th century) manipulated square roots of negatives formally; Bombelli clarified rules for these quantities. The geometric picture as a plane is associated with Argand and Wessel (early 19th century), with Gauss also contributing to acceptance. Hamilton later placed complex numbers on a rigorous footing as pairs of reals with a defined multiplication.

The modern view — complex analysis, Riemann surfaces, quantum theory — treats \mathbb{C} as indispensable, not a trick for “impossible” square roots.
