Vectors in the plane

A beginner’s guide: components, length, addition, scaling, and the dot product—with live diagrams

What is a vector (here)?

In the plane, a vector is a quantity with two pieces of information: how far you move left–right and how far you move up–down. We write it as an ordered pair of real numbers, for example \mathbf{v} = (v_x,\, v_y), or as a column vector:

\displaystyle \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \end{pmatrix}

You can picture \mathbf{v} as an arrow from the origin (0,0) to the point (v_x, v_y). The same pair can mean a displacement (“go v_x in x, then v_y in y”) even if you start somewhere other than the origin—then you draw the same arrow starting at that point.

This page sticks to two dimensions so everything fits on one screen. The same ideas extend to 3D with a third component v_z.

Length (magnitude)

The length (or magnitude) of \mathbf{v} is written |\mathbf{v}| or sometimes \|\mathbf{v}\|. By Pythagoras in the right triangle with legs |v_x| and |v_y|, the slanted side has length:

\displaystyle |\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}

A vector of length 1 is a unit vector. Dividing any nonzero vector by its length makes a unit vector pointing the same way:

\displaystyle \hat{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|}

Adding vectors

Algebraically, add component by component:

\displaystyle (u_x,\, u_y) + (v_x,\, v_y) = (u_x + v_x,\; u_y + v_y)

Geometrically, place the tail of \mathbf{v} at the tip of \mathbf{u}; the arrow from the origin to that tip is \mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v} (tip-to-tail rule). Equivalently, build the parallelogram with sides \mathbf{u} and \mathbf{v} from the origin; the diagonal from the origin is again \mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}.

Multiplying a vector by a number (scalar multiplication)

A scalar is an ordinary real number (not bold). Multiplying a vector \mathbf{v} by a scalar c scales every component:

\displaystyle c\,(v_x,\, v_y) = (c\,v_x,\; c\,v_y)

Geometry: if c > 1, the arrow gets longer in the same direction; if 0 \lt c \lt 1, it shrinks; if c \lt 0, it flips to the opposite direction and scales by |c|. In every case, |c\,\mathbf{v}| = |c|\,|\mathbf{v}|.

Multiplying two vectors: the dot product

There is no single “product of two vectors” that returns another arrow in the plane without extra structure. The most important product for beginners is the dot product (scalar product), which takes two vectors and returns one number:

\displaystyle \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = u_x v_x + u_y v_y

Geometry: if \theta is the angle between \mathbf{u} and \mathbf{v} when they start at the same point, then

\displaystyle \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}|\,|\mathbf{v}|\,\cos\theta

So the dot product measures how much one vector lies along the other: it is large and positive when they point nearly the same way, zero when they are perpendicular, and negative when they point more opposite than not.

In 2D you may also meet the 2×2 determinant u_x v_y - u_y v_x, which gives the signed area of the parallelogram spanned by \mathbf{u} and \mathbf{v}—related to “cross product” ideas in 3D. The diagrams above already let you see when that parallelogram is wide or nearly flat (parallel vectors).

Standard basis

The vectors \mathbf{i} = (1,0) and \mathbf{j} = (0,1) point along the axes and have length 1. Any vector in the plane can be written \mathbf{v} = v_x\,\mathbf{i} + v_y\,\mathbf{j}—the same numbers as its components.

Related note

Complex numbers a+bi add exactly like vectors (a,b) in the plane; multiplication is an extra twist (rotation and scaling). See Complex numbers — a visual brief.
